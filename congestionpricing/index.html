How congestion pricing could improve health

In Fall 2021, the Metropolitan Transportation Authority began the process of implementing a Central Business District Tolling Program, also known as congestion pricing.

Through this plan, cars and trucks that enter Manhattan below 60th street - the Central Business District - would be charged a toll.

This money would be used to fund important improvements to New York City’s transit system of subways, busses, and railroads - maintaining and modernizing the system to provide more capacity, reliability, and accessibility.

Quick facts from MTA.info:

  • About 700,000 vehicles enter the Central Business District each day.
  • The average traffic speed in the Central Business District is only 7 miles per hour - and has been getting worse over the past decade.
  • Each New Yorker, on average, loses 133 hours due to traffic - and nearly $1,900 in lost productivity and other costs!

Traffic is a huge part of our environment in New York City. Congestion pricing, as a strategy to affect traffic density, could change the ways that our environment affects our health. Let’s look at some ways it might do this:

Walking, driving, and cycling

In the Central Business District, less than 1 out of every 4 trips uses a car. Explore more data on modal use.

That means that despite the ubiquity of car traffic, most New Yorkers don’t drive regularly. A 2016 estimate found that only 40% of New Yorkers reported using a car in the past month. Twice as many walked or cycled for transportation in the same timeframe. Explore data here.

This is good news for health! Studies show that active mobility reduces mortality since more time walking and cycling is good for you.

Traffic density

Despite car traffic being a way that a minority of New Yorkers travel, it has a huge impact on our environment.

A 2016 estimate found that in NYC, each square kilometer sees an average of 21.7 million miles of vehicular travel per year. It’s highest in Manhattan, with a boro average of 43,700,000 miles per square kilometer - or over 110 million miles traveled per square mile. Explore more data on traffic density.

Traffic density affects Air Quality.

In NYC, a neighborhood’s air quality is related to its level of building emissions, building density, industrial areas, and traffic density. Explore data on air quality, and explore air quality in your neighborhood.

When we looked at a few examples, we found that air quality improves from traffic reductions. Read more at Air quality during COVID-19, and Air quality in a car-free Times Square. Congestion pricing may not reduce traffic enough to have a dramatic effect on air pollution, and other neighborhoods in NYC are higher-priority air quality neighborhoods. However, reducing traffic density (and thus emissions) through congestion pricing would benefit the city, given the health impacts of air pollution and health impacts due to traffic.

Can lowering traffic volume affect street safety?

In New York City, many people are concerned about traffic-related injuries - a preventable public health issue that affects too many New Yorkers each year. Explore data on pedestrian and bicycling injuries here.

Traffic density is just one component of many environmental variables that can affect street safety - we explored more of those here.

The streetscape is a huge part of our city, and as part of our environment, it can encourage or inhibit other healthy behaviors that keep us healthy. Explore neighborhood-level data on active design and physical activity here.

The benefits of transit

A strong public transit system is vital for an equitable city. Since 1 out of every 8 New York City residents has a mobility impairment, an accessible, modern system should be a priority.

According to data from the American Community Survey, only 1 out of 4 NYC residents use cars to commute. These car-owning New Yorkers have an average income that’s more than double that of households that don’t own cars (source). So how do the rest of New Yorkers get around? Transit.

Congestion pricing will fund this modernization. It promises to take vehicular traffic, which imposes a cost on everyone, and convert it into funding for transit, which benefits us all.

New York City’s transit system moves millions of people per day - far more than private vehicles. It is one of the oldest, longest, largest, and most-used transit systems in the world. It is, without a doubt, one of New York City’s crown jewels, and its ability to move people around the city efficiently powers New York City’s economy on a fundamental level.

Credit: Mayoral Photograpy Office

But its benefits are more than economic. One of the greenest things you can do is to use transit for traveling. Greenhouse gas emissions are driving changes to the climate, and we expect more frequent and more severe storms, extreme heat events, and other weather-related crises.

In 2012, Superstorm Sandy showed us that a storm can damage our city’s transit system; this year, Hurricane Ida reminded us that powerful storms can be deadly. But more commonly, extreme heat kills more people in New York City than all other natural disasters combined. Explore the factors that affect heat vulnerability.

Congestion pricing touches on big issues of environmental health: traffic, air quality, street safety, active design, transit use, and climate change. We know just how important transit usage is to handle climate change. This means that a healthy, robust transit system is vital to the sustainability and health of our city as we move into an uncertain, dynamic, and potentially dangerous future.
