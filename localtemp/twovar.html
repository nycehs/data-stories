What cools your block?

The urban heat island effect, and what it means for your neighborhood

At 6.00 PM on June 30 2018, the air temperature at the La Guardia Airport Weather Station was 89 degrees Fahrenheit.

At the same time, the air temperature on a block in East Harlem (below left) was 93.9 degrees - nearly 5 degrees hotter than at the LGA weather station. The air temperature on the block in Bed-Stuy (below right) was 92.5 degrees: cooler, but still 3.5 degrees hotter than at the weather station.

This is an example of the Urban Heat Island Effect: when cities are hotter than surrounding areas. In cities, more surfaces like roofs and roads absorb heat, building boilers and car engines generate additional heat, and there's limited vegetation to cool things down.

As the pictures above illustrate, temperatures vary from block to block within a city. Characteristics of different areas contribute to their temperature differences.

What cools your block?

Anyone who has looked for a shady spot under a tree during a hot sunny day knows that trees help to cool off a street. But by how much? How effective are trees at cooling neighborhoods, and what other characteristics contribute to an area’s air temperature during hot weather?

To find out how much different characteristics affect "hyperlocal temperature" we combined temperature data with data on neighborhood factors. Read more about our methods.

We installed over 500 air temperature sensors in 16 different neighborhoods, and collected a temperature reading every 10 minutes from June to September in 2018 and 2019.

Then, we collected data from a variety of sources, including sattelite imagery, to determine things like building height, tree canopy coverage, green space, and impervious surfaces for each place where we were monitoring temperature.

We used this data to develop a model that analyses the temperature data to determine the effect that different factors have on hyperlocal temperature.

Tree canopy coverage

The percent of total tree canopy coverage on the sidewalk in the right of way of the monitored street. Trees provide shade and prevent the ground from heating up. They also can cool the air through evapotranspiration. Evapotranspiration occurs when the sun’s rays hit plants, causing water to evaporate from the leaves. This change from liquid to vapor cools the plants – just as sweating cools our bodies – and uses energy thereby reducing the amount of energy left to warm the air.

Grass and shrubs

The percent of grass and shrubs on the sidewalk of the monitored street. Grass and shrubs cool the air through evapotranspiration and shading.

Building height

Our model looks at the median building height (the middle range of sorted values) of buildings on the block. Taller buildings can absorb and reflect more heat (increasing the street's temperature), but also shade the street to cool it.

Impervious surfaces

Impervious surfaces are made from asphalt, concrete, astroturf, even hard packed dirt. These surfaces absorb sunlight and reflect and retain heat. They increase the temperature of the block.

Other factors

Other factors, like the orientation of the street (North/South or East/West), how close the area is to the ocean, the elevation of the area, daily wind speed and weather patterns can also influence how hot a block is. These other factors are hard to quantify reliably, but can affect variation in hyperlocal temperature.

Each of these factors affect hyperlocal temperature - by either helping to cool a block, or by retaining heat and preventing cooling. But the most important factor? Green space. More tree canopy, or coverage by grass and shrubs, reduces temperatures the most.

Based on 2018 data, we modelled the predicted average summer temperature from 6 PM to 9 PM, and how it would change based on different factors. This time frame is important - it's when the Urban Heat Island Effect causes the highest temperature differences as the sun sets but the city retains heat.

More greenspace means lower temperatures. But the ability of trees, grass, and shrubs to cool a neighborhood is reduced when we add other factors - like impervious surfaces. Driveways and sidewalks absorb sunlight and reflect and retain heat. They increase the temperature, and reduce area available to convert into greenspace.

So while our models show the potential for a 7-degree reduction in temperature from tree canopy and grass and shrub coverage, once we add impervious surfaces into the model, we see the potential for only about 2 degrees of cooling.

This may sound small, but...

Cooling a block by an average of 2 degrees may not sound like much, but small differences in temperature - especially at peak summer temperatures - can mean the difference bewteen small numbers and large numbers of heat-related emergency department visits, and heat-related deaths.

After all, hot weather is dangerous. In New York City and across the country, more people die from extreme heat than from all other natural disasters combined. And, NYC is more vulnerable to heat than suburban areas.

That means that rising average temperatures and heat waves are even hotter in the city. These place New Yorkers at risk: when we analyzed excess mortality during extreme heat events, we estimated that over 110 New Yorkers died each year due to extreme heat (1997 – 2013).

When we distill the data to understand how these different factors affect temperature, the extent to which greenspace or tree canopy alone can cool a neighborhood may seem small – a few degrees. But this small change can have a significant impact: the risk for heat-related illness rises exponentially around 90 degrees. Even modest cooling can save lives, especially as we anticipate increasing frequency and intensity of extreme heat events as the climate changes.
